## What Must Be on a US Invoice?

Unlike the UK, EU, or Australia, the United States has no federal law requiring a specific invoice format. However, standard business practice and state tax rules mean a compliant US invoice should include:

| Field | Required? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Your business name and address | Yes (practical) | Needed for your client's records |
| Client name and address | Yes (practical) | Needed for your records |
| Invoice number | Recommended | For tracking and disputes |
| Invoice date | Yes | Determines payment deadline |
| Due date / payment terms | Recommended | Net 15, Net 30, etc. |
| Description of services or goods | Yes | Be specific enough for tax purposes |
| Quantity and rate | Yes | Line-item breakdown |
| Subtotal | Yes | Before tax |
| Sales tax (if applicable) | Conditional | See below |

| Payment instructions | Recommended | Bank transfer, check, PayPal, etc. |

There's no legal requirement in the US to use the word "Invoice" on the document, but it's standard practice.

## How Sales Tax Works in the US

Sales tax in the US is state-level, not federal. Key points:

- **Not all services are taxable** — in most states, professional services (consulting, design, software development) are exempt from sales tax. Physical goods are usually taxable.
- **Rates vary by state**: California ranges from 7.25% to 10.75% (with local additions), Texas is 8.25%, Florida is 7%, New York is 8.875% in NYC, Oregon has no sales tax at all.
- **Nexus matters**: You're only required to collect sales tax in states where you have a "nexus" — usually where your business is physically located, or where you exceed a sales threshold (typically $100,000/year or 200 transactions in that state under post-*Wayfair* rules).
- **If in doubt, consult a tax professional** — the rules are genuinely complex and vary significantly.

## Common US Invoice Mistakes

**1. Not including a due date**
"Due on receipt" is not specific enough. Use "Net 15" (due 15 days after invoice date) or "Net 30" — it makes following up on late payments much easier.

**2. Charging sales tax on exempt services**
If you're a freelance developer or consultant, most states don't require you to collect sales tax on services. Charging it when you don't have to is incorrect. Research your state's rules.

**3. No payment method specified**
Always include how you want to be paid — bank transfer (include routing + account number), PayPal/Venmo, check payable to, etc. Ambiguity delays payment.

**4. Invoice number gaps**
Sequential, gapless invoice numbers matter for accounting. If you void INV-003, note it — don't just skip to INV-004 in your records.

**5. No late payment terms**
Consider adding a line: "A late fee of 1.5% per month applies to invoices not paid within 30 days." This is legally enforceable in most states and speeds up payment.
