## The Problem With Most PDF to JPG Tools

Most online converters follow the same playbook. You upload your PDF to their server, wait for it to process, download the images, and hope the quality is decent.

A few issues with that:

**Your file lives on their server.**

When you upload a PDF, it goes to a cloud server you don't control. Legal contracts, financial statements, medical records, business documents — these might sit in someone's storage bucket for hours, days, or longer.

**Quality is often reduced.**

Server-side conversions frequently compress images to save storage and bandwidth. You get a blurry, low-resolution JPG that looks nothing like the original PDF page.

**Free tiers have limits.**

Watermarks, page limits, conversion caps, required signup. The "free" tools often aren't really free.

## Why 2x Quality Matters

Most PDF to JPG converters render pages at standard resolution — what you see on screen. The problem is that screen resolution (72–96 DPI) looks terrible when printed or displayed at larger sizes.

This tool renders each page at 2x scale, which produces images at roughly 144–192 DPI. That means:

- Text stays sharp when you zoom in
- Charts and diagrams retain their detail
- Images are large enough to use in presentations without looking pixelated
- Quality is closer to print-ready than screen-ready

## JPG vs PNG — Which Should You Choose?

**Choose JPG when:**

- The PDF page contains photos or complex graphics
- File size matters (email attachments, web uploads)
- You'll be sharing on social media or messaging apps
- The page has a lot of color or gradients

**Choose PNG when:**

- The PDF page is mostly text or line art
- You need sharp edges on logos, diagrams, or charts
- You plan to edit the image further
- Maximum quality is more important than file size
