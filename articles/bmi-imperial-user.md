## The Imperial BMI Formula

For those who want to understand the math:

**BMI = (weight in pounds × 703) ÷ (height in inches)²**

Example: 5'9" (69 inches), 160 lbs

BMI = (160 × 703) ÷ (69²) = 112,480 ÷ 4,761 = **23.6**

The 703 factor is the conversion constant that makes the imperial formula produce the same BMI values as the metric formula.

## What BMI Doesn't Tell You

BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnostic one. Two important limitations:

**It doesn't distinguish muscle from fat.** A muscular athlete and someone with high body fat can have the same BMI. BMI at 27 means very different things for a competitive cyclist and a sedentary office worker.

**It uses the same scale for all body types.** Research shows BMI thresholds may underestimate health risk for South Asian and East Asian populations, and overestimate it for Black populations. Some health organisations use adjusted thresholds for different ethnic groups.

Use BMI as a starting point for a conversation with a doctor — not as a definitive health verdict.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is BMI calculated differently for men and women?**
No. The standard BMI formula is the same for men and women. Some alternative assessments (like body fat percentage) do differ by sex, but BMI itself doesn't.

**What BMI is considered healthy?**
18.5 to 24.9 is the standard "normal weight" range. However, athletic individuals with high muscle mass often fall in the "overweight" range (25–29.9) despite having low body fat.

**How often should I check my BMI?**
BMI is a long-term metric — checking monthly is sufficient. Daily weight fluctuations (water retention, meals) can shift BMI by 0.5–1 point without any meaningful change in body composition.

**Does height need to be exact?**
Half-inch differences in height produce small BMI changes (typically less than 0.2 BMI points). Being close is sufficient for screening purposes.
