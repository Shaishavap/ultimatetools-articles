## Why Daily Sudoku Works as a Habit

There's a reason newspapers carry a daily Sudoku and the New York Times built an empire on daily puzzles. The constraint of "one puzzle per day" creates three useful things at once:

- **A clear start and end** — you're not browsing infinite content, you're solving one specific thing
- **Shared difficulty** — when your friend says "today's medium was brutal", you know what they mean
- **A bounded reward loop** — solve it, feel good, close the tab and get on with your day

That bounded reward is the part most apps get wrong. They want infinite engagement, so they generate infinite puzzles, so the reward never really lands. A daily puzzle is the opposite design — finite, scoped, complete.

This Sudoku page does both. The daily is the front door. Random puzzles are there when you want them but they're framed as the alternative, not the default.

## How to Play Sudoku

Fill every row, column, and 3×3 box with the digits 1–9 exactly once. That's it. Two cells in the same row can't share a number. Same for columns and boxes.

Mechanics in this tool:

- **Tap a cell, then a number** on the on-screen keypad or your keyboard
- **Notes (pencil) mode** — tap the pencil icon to enter small candidate numbers in a cell, useful for tracking possibilities without committing
- **Conflict highlighting** — wrong placements turn red so you catch errors immediately
- **Hint button** — limited hints reveal one correct cell from the solution if you're stuck
- **Auto-pause notes** — when you fill a cell, candidate notes in the same row/column/box for that number are automatically cleared

The grid is responsive, works equally well on desktop and mobile. Touch and click both work. There is no clock pressure unless you want it — a small timer runs in the corner so you can compare solve times day to day, but it never forces a fail state.

## What "Daily" Means Technically

The daily puzzle is **deterministic by date**. The puzzle generator uses a mulberry32 seeded pseudo-random number generator with today's calendar date (YYYYMMDD format) as the seed. That seed plus a fixed difficulty offset produces the same puzzle layout for everyone on the same calendar day, in any timezone.

Tomorrow, the seed changes, and a new puzzle is generated. There are no servers involved — everything runs in your browser. That means there's nothing to fail, no signup that could expire, no API that could break. As long as the page loads, you get today's puzzle.

This also means your progress is saved locally per device. If you open the page on your phone and then later on your laptop, you'll see the same puzzle but with the laptop's fresh empty grid. That's a feature, not a bug — your phone progress isn't shared, so you don't accidentally see your own solution mid-grid.
