# BMI Calculator — What Your Number Means and How to Use It

BMI (Body Mass Index) is one of the most used health metrics in the world — and one of the most misunderstood. It's a single number that classifies your weight relative to your height. Knowing what it means, and what it doesn't, makes it actually useful.

## How BMI Is Calculated

The formula is the same regardless of which system you use:

**Metric:** BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²

**Imperial:** BMI = (weight (lbs) ÷ height (inches)²) × 703

You don't need to do this math manually — the calculator handles both. Enter your weight and height, select metric or imperial, and the result is instant.

## The WHO Categories

The World Health Organization defines four BMI ranges:

| BMI Range | Category |
|---|---|
| Below 18.5 | Underweight |
| 18.5 – 24.9 | Normal weight |
| 25.0 – 29.9 | Overweight |
| 30.0 and above | Obese |

These categories are the global standard used in clinical settings, health surveys, and insurance assessments.

## What BMI Does Not Tell You

BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. It has known limitations:

**Muscle mass:** BMI doesn't distinguish between fat and muscle. A trained athlete with high muscle mass may show as "overweight" despite low body fat. This is why BMI is less useful for bodybuilders or very athletic individuals.

**Age and sex:** The same BMI value means different things at different ages. For children and adolescents, age- and sex-adjusted BMI-for-age percentiles are used instead of the adult categories.

**Fat distribution:** Where fat is stored matters as much as how much. Abdominal fat carries higher cardiovascular risk than fat stored in the hips or thighs — BMI doesn't capture this distinction.

**Ethnicity:** Research suggests health risks begin at lower BMI thresholds for some Asian populations. Some organizations use adjusted cutoffs — for example, overweight starting at BMI 23 for South Asian adults.

## When BMI Is Useful

Despite its limitations, BMI remains the standard first-pass health metric because it's fast, free, and requires only weight and height — no equipment, no lab work.

It's useful for:
- Tracking weight change over time (same person, consistent measurement method)
- Population-level health assessments
- A starting point before more detailed body composition analysis
- Standard clinical and insurance screening

It's a signal, not a verdict. Use it alongside other indicators — waist circumference, resting heart rate, energy levels — for a fuller picture.

---

## How to Track Progress Over Time

If you're using BMI to track health changes:

1. Weigh yourself at the same time each day (morning before eating is most consistent)
2. Use the same units throughout — don't switch between metric and imperial
3. Check monthly, not daily — weight naturally fluctuates 1–3 lbs day to day from water, food, and other factors
4. Track the trend over 3–6 months, not the individual reading

A BMI shift of 0.5–1.0 points over three months reflects genuine change. A 0.3-point overnight shift is normal body fluctuation.
